About Me

I am interested in how machine learning and computational methods can help us understand and improve social and behavioral outcomes at the population scale.

I received an M.S. in Data Analytics Engineering from Northeastern University, and my training bridges research, data science, and software engineering.

I am looking for research assistant opportunities and collaborations in machine learning, real-world data, and related problems in behavior, prediction, and decision-making.

Research Experience

1. Modeling behavioral signals from structured survey data.

Survey questions are often connected and organized around related topics. I experimented with whether richer deep learning models could capture these dependencies better than strong tabular baselines. I also analyzed attention maps and feature attribution results to understand what the model had learned and when learned representations were actually useful for downstream prediction, especially in the STI setting (BMC'26) where gender differences and psychosocial pressures shape how risk is expressed and reported.

2. Learning under messy data.

In real-world problems I have worked on, the data were often incomplete, noisy, or limited in sample size, and sometimes the prediction targets themselves were delayed or under-observed. Working with this kind of data pushed me toward problems like missing-data imputation, domain adaptation, and prediction across cohorts under distribution shift (Nat Commun'25).

3. From prediction to inference and decision support.

I also worked on how model outputs could support population-level inference, risk stratification, and real-world decision-making (JTM'24). This came up in different projects, such as studying geographic clustering and delayed diagnosis (ongoing), and building tools that could be used more directly in practice (Nat Commun'25).
