About Me

I am a researcher working at the intersection of computational social science, machine learning, and public-interest data analysis. My work focuses on using large-scale survey, administrative, and real-world datasets to study human behavior, decision-making, and population-level risk.

I am particularly interested in how computational methods can reveal social and behavioral patterns, support policy- and decision-relevant questions, and remain useful under the messiness of real-world data. My background combines research and engineering: I received an M.S. in Data Analytics Engineering from Northeastern University, and I have worked across research, data science, and software engineering roles.

Research Interests

  • Computational social science
  • Human behavior and decision-making
  • Social and behavioral risk modeling
  • Population-level prediction with survey and administrative data
  • Spatial and temporal pattern analysis
  • Robust machine learning for real-world data

Selected Projects

Social and Behavioral Risk Modeling for STI Prediction

Developed predictive models on large-scale survey data to study factors associated with STI risk, with a focus on how social norms, privacy, and behavioral factors appear in model signals.

Decision-Making and Predictive Models in Public Health

Studied how predictive models can support decision-making for individuals, providers, and policymakers, especially in settings where risk, uncertainty, and practical action need to be considered together.

Spatial and Population-Level Analysis of Late HIV Diagnosis

Worked on population-based analyses of late HIV diagnosis, focusing on spatial clustering, geographic heterogeneity, and factors associated with delayed diagnosis.
